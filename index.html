Abstract

Computer science (CS) education has become a popular choice for students with different native languages and yet most computer programming languages, course materials, and educational environments continue to be based on the English language. This paper identifies challenges faced by non-native English speakers (NNES) related to studying computer science through an analysis of several studies concerning programming transfer, documentation comprehension, terminology knowledge, and cognitive load; the results indicate NNES experience difficulties with understanding programming concepts, interpreting error messages, and transitioning to text-based programming due to limitations with their English language skills, lack of familiarity with technical terminology, and dependency of the programming languages syntax on English. Additionally, this paper uses a table from a multi-case study conducted by Alaofi (2025) to identify the most common English language problems. The final portion of this paper provides suggestions for improving teaching methods and course materials to assist in reducing inequities and increasing participation in computing by a broader audience.

Introduction

English has become an increasingly dominant programming language globally, despite the rapid development of education of computing around the world (Guo, 2018). To non-English native speaker (NNES) students, this is a huge barrier. Beyond basic syntax and logical thinking, NNES have to interpret unfamiliar terms, follow English language instruction in class and be able to understand the error message written for English native speakers (Becker, 2019). Programming languages are embedded with English grammar and vocabulary, thus, the ability to speak English will significantly affect the student's ability to succeed in introductory courses (Becker, 2019). CS1 classes typically assume English fluency for students regardless of whether English is used in daily educational settings (Alaofi, 2025). Understanding these difficulties is critical for developing an inclusive education model for CS.

Language Hurdles in Learning Programming

1.1 Structure and Terms Specific to the Language

Programming languages such as Python, Java, and C++ include English specific words and phrases such as "if", "else" and "return" that do not translate meaningfully into other languages (Becker, 2019). Guo (2018) identified that NNES face challenges related to reading instructional material and programming elements including identifiers and abbreviations. Alaofi (2025) noted that a lack of knowledge regarding common computer terminology was highly correlated with poor CS1 performance. Prather et al. (2017) demonstrated that learners utilize natural language reasoning when reading code, and that NNES have less effective reasoning than English native speakers.

1.2 Increased Cognitive Overload

The additional mental burden of having to overcome linguistic barriers adds to the cognitive overload experienced by NNES while solving logical programming problems (Espinal et al., 2023). Translating code into a student's native language uses up the working memory needed to solve programming problems. Also, the fear of asking questions incorrectly due to not being proficient causes NNES to refrain from seeking help. Espinal et al. (2023) documented how NNES students were unable to effectively describe code based on English structures and expressions during their transition from Scratch to Python.

1.3 Barriers to Class Communication

Alaofi (2025) found that students had difficulty asking questions in class due to fear of incorrect English usage, reducing opportunities for assistance. Similarly, Guo (2018) noted that students were hesitant to engage in online discussion forums due to fear of misinterpreting technical English or using incorrect terminology.

Reported English-Related Barriers Affecting CS1 Learning

Alaofi (2025) conducted a study involving over 300 students from China and Saudi Arabia to identify how English proficiency, terminology knowledge, and anxiety affect CS1 performance. Results from the Chinese case study (Table 1 and Figure 1) identified four primary English-related barriers.

According to Table 1, 50% of students encountered difficulty reading English, 27% encountered difficulty with CS-specific vocabulary, 23% had difficulty understanding programming concepts in English, and 20% struggled to translate English-based programs into their native language. Smaller percentages (13–10%) reported difficulty with English-based assignments and online materials. Figure 1 shows that 28–30% of students struggled with English-based error messages, IDE interfaces, and help commands. The findings indicate four major barriers: limited English proficiency, difficulty interpreting programming terminology, difficulty reading error messages, and difficulty applying English in CS contexts.

Improving CS Education for NNES Students

To develop and support NNES learners, explicit support for terminology is necessary; terminology knowledge predicts performance in CS1 (Alaofi, 2025). Vocabulary activities, bilingual glossaries, simplified definitions, or inline dictionaries may support interpretation of documentation (Guo, 2018). Reducing cognitive load through visual tools, bilingual comments, and paired natural-language/code examples can also help (Espinal et al., 2023). Low-stakes participation and inclusive, translated materials may help NNES learners engage more confidently.

Conclusions

Language barriers influence how NNES understand terminology, interpret error messages, participate in class, and transition into text-based programming. These barriers arise from structural linguistic dominance rather than learner inadequacy. Improved course design, multilingual materials, and terminology scaffolding can reduce cognitive burden and support more equitable computing education.
